### Chapter 1 — Title
The apartment was the kind of quiet that happens after ten o'clock on a Tuesday in a city that is otherwise always making noise: the freeway a low surf in the distance, the upstairs neighbor's television a muffled heartbeat through the ceiling, the refrigerator cycling on and then off again with the particular domestic sigh of something that has been keeping things cold for a very long time. Derek Krishnamurthy sat at his desk in the amber cone of an Elgato key light, and he was by all accounts, to the eighty-seven people watching him through the internet, having the time of his life.

"Okay, okay, okay. Listen," he said, leaning forward so that his dark eyes caught the light in a way that looked practiced but was in fact not, "I need you all to understand that Robocop is not a movie about a robot. That is a surface-level reading that the studio used to sell tickets and that the culture has been lazy enough to accept for forty years. Robocop is a movie about identity. What survives when everything that makes you inconvenient has been stripped away." He paused, picked up his mug of tea, and let the pause breathe the way a good teacher lets silence be it's own event. "Murphy is the empathy. The machine is the institution. The whole film is asking: what does a corporation do to a human soul? And we keep calling it the one with the robot cop."

In the chat, the names scrolled:

> *GunplaGuy88: ok but the ED-209 walking scene alone is cinema*

> *Lumen442: what about terminator 2 tho*

"Lumen," Derek said, reading the screen with the ease of someone who had learned to carry on two conversations simultaneously without losing the thread of either, "Terminator 2 is a masterpiece and I will defend it until they put me in the ground, but that's a different thesis. That's about the the line between a human who has been stripped of empathy meeting a war machine learning it for the first time. Tonight we're in the Verhoeven lane. Stay in the Verhoeven lane with me."

He genuinely laughed at something in the chat that had already scrolled past, and a few of the eighty-seven people watching him in their own dark rooms in their own cities probably felt, for a moment, that they were in on something.

This was what Derek was good at. This specific thing.

He had known it since his first year teaching, when he'd stood in front of a class of ninth graders who had every cultural and hormonal reason to be somewhere else, and had managed, through the particular alchemy of caring about the material more than he cared about appearing cool, to make them care too. He could thread a room. He could find the live wire in a text and follow it until the light came on behind someone's eyes. In a different era, in a different family, this might have been called a gift. In his family it was called talking too much.

He ended the stream at eleven forty-two. He said his goodbyes the way he always did, unhurried, actually looking at each of the names as he said them.

"Goodnight FilmNerd_Priya. Goodnight GunplaGuy. Lumen, I love you but you are wrong about Ant-Man and we've discussed this. Everyone else: genuinely, thank you. This is the good part of my week."

He meant it. That was the thing he would have had trouble explaining to anyone who didn't already understand it: he meant it completely. It was also not the whole truth.

The stream ended. The key light stayed on for a moment, as it always did, and then he reached up and clicked it.  The room reassembled itself in the ordinary lamplight of a man's apartment at midnight: the shelves of steelbooks in their obsessive rows, spines facing out like the spines of a library curated by someone with a specific and unapologetic inner life. The Criterion editions. The art prints in their matching frames: a Mondo illustration of Chinatown, a Bottleneck Gallery print of Mulholland Drive, a hand-numbered Ghost in the Shell variant he had paid too much for and did not even slightly regret. On the shelf beside them, in a modest disorder: a Raspberry Pi mid-project, a stack of New Yorkers he was always six issues behind on, a ring dish from his ex-wife's mother that he had kept because it was beautiful and she had given it to him before everything had become what it became.

He did not look at the ring dish. He had become an expert at not looking at it.

He opened the refrigerator, found it in its usual state: half a container of leftover dal, some yogurt, a mango in a state of ambiguous ripeness, and a bottle of Topo Chico.  He poured the mineral water into a glass with the slightly ceremonial attention he gave to small things when no one was watching, which was one of the ways the gap opened between the version of himself he broadcast and the version that remained.

The broadcast version was confident. He was confident. Both things were true, but they were not the same size.

He sat back down at the desk, not to work, just because the chair was there and the lamp was warm and the alternative was the bedroom, which at midnight had a quality of accusation he was not in the mood to negotiate with. He pulled up YouTube, not to look for anything, just the reflexive motion of a man who had learned to be slightly afraid of his own silence.

Maya Chen's face appeared in the recommended feed the way it always eventually did. He clicked before making the decision about clicking.

The video was titled: "I tried to explain parasocial relationships to my mom (she did not get it)" and it had four point two million views.

Maya Chen was the biggest female streamer in the world. She had eleven million subscribers on YouTube, four million on Twitch, a podcast, a merchandise line, and the kind of online presence that had achieved critical mass.  In other words, she was at the point at which the person and the persona become, to the external world, interchangeable.

Derek clicked play.

She was sitting on her couch in what appeared to be a well-lit but deliberately casual arrangement, the kind that costs a significant amount to look effortless, and she was laughing at something her editor had cut in, and the laugh was the thing Derek had noticed the first time he'd watched her content and the thing that had made him keep watching. It was not a streamer laugh. It arrived sideways, unannounced, the kind of laugh that happens when something is genuinely funny rather than when you are performing the experience of something being funny. He had spent enough time in front of a camera to recognize the difference.

"She kept asking me," Maya was saying, wiping at her eye with the heel of her hand, "she kept asking me, 'But are they your real friends?' "And she looked at me and she said, 'Maya, I think you need real friends.' And I was like, okay. Okay, Mom. Thank you. Very helpful."

She said it without bitterness, a light smile on her face. There was a quality to the way she said it, head cocked, the absurdity and the genuine sting held at the same time, that Derek found himself watching twice.

He closed the laptop at twelve forty.

He lay in bed with the ceiling above him and the familiar weight that was not quite sadness and not quite loneliness but lived in the same neighborhood as both, and he thought about what she'd said.  The real friends question, and about the eighty-seven people who had watched him tonight and the warmth he had felt for them, and the specific silence that waited when the stream ended, just him and the ring dish and the mango of ambiguous ripeness.

He was thirty-five years old. He had been cheated on by everyone he'd loved enough to stay with. He lived alone in an apartment full of things he had chosen with enormous care, which is what you do when the people you chose did not choose you back in the same way. He taught English to teenagers who would grow up to remember him, if they remembered him at all, as the teacher who made them read things they hadn't wanted to read and who had, when they finally read them, been right about the thing that those things were about.

He was not unhappy. He needed to be precise about this, because imprecision in the other direction was its own kind of dishonesty. Not unhappy. The particular condition of a person who has arranged their life with considerable intelligence and care and arrived at something almost but not quite sufficient.

He had been doing the thing where you hold open a space and wonder what might eventually fill it, patient and undestructive, for two years now: since Priya, since the divorce, since the winter after that he didn't discuss.

He had built things since then: the podcast, the classroom, the apartment full of chosen things, and the things were real and they mattered and they were not the same as having someone to call when you found the Coltrane pressing you had been looking for.

He looked at the ring dish on the shelf. He had kept it because it was beautiful and because she had given it to him before everything became what it became, and he had kept it and not thought about why he had kept it, and on some nights, like tonight, the keeping of it said something about him that he was not fully ready to examine.

He fell asleep, eventually, with the fan on and the city continuing its muffled business outside and the ring dish on the shelf and the wondering in the room, patient, as it always was, and uninterested in being hurried.


### Chapter 2 — Title

Maya Chen had a pre-stream ritual that took forty-seven minutes. She had never described it to anyone. Not because it was secret (it wasn't, particularly) but because describing it would make it strange, and she needed it to not be strange. Just a thing she did. Neutral and repeatable.

Like brushing your teeth.

She started with the ring light off and the room in the ordinary afternoon light of her apartment in Burbank, which was the color of November regardless of what month it actually was. She made tea, always loose leaf, always in the same blue ceramic pot her grandmother had sent from Guangzhou two Christmases ago, and she sat with it at the island in her kitchen and she did not look at her phone. This was the hardest part.

This was the part that required the most discipline, because the phone was a current and if she stepped into it before she was ready she would arrive at the stream already depleted, already having spent the first increment of herself on someone else's emergency.

She was twenty-six years old. She had been doing this since she was nineteen. She had grown up in the camera.

At forty minutes she went to the second bedroom, the stream room.

No one who visited her apartment ever entered it. No one who visited her apartment was someone she'd met in person before they'd met her online.

She knew this was a fact about her life. She was still deciding how she felt about it. She sat in the chair and looked at the dark monitor and the offline camera and she breathed.

Then she turned the ring light on. And she became, in the illumination, slightly more than herself.

Not fake. She was careful about this distinction. A version of herself: the one that had learned, through seven years of practice, which parts of her were interesting and how to lead with those parts without abandoning the rest. Every person did this. Every person walked into a room and calibrated. The difference was merely the scale.

"Hello hello hello," she said to the camera as the stream went live, and the chat exploded in its predictable and delightful way, three thousand people arriving in the first sixty seconds, the number climbing toward fifteen thousand as the notification rippled out through her community. She smiled, and the smile was real but it was also the smile she had developed specifically for this: slightly warmer than her ordinary smile, slightly more open, the smile of someone who is genuinely glad to see you and has practiced being genuinely glad.

"Okay so I lied," she said immediately, settling into the chair with her tea, "I said we were going to play Balatro tonight but I got into a thing earlier and I kind of want to just talk for a little bit first. Is that okay?"

The chat said it was okay. The chat was always okay with whatever Maya wanted to do, which was something she appreciated and something she was aware was a form of power that required responsible management.

"So I had this conversation with my mom," she said, and she told the story, not the same story as the YouTube video but a different iteration, differently angled, and as she talked she was watching the chat and reading it and responding to it simultaneously, which was a skill she had developed the way pianists develop the ability to read and play simultaneously, until it was no longer effort but just the shape of attention.

"She said, she looked at me, and I love my mom, she's like, the best, but she said..." Maya paused for effect, and also because the pause was genuinely necessary, because even now the memory had a small hook in it, "'Maya, I think you need real friends.'" In the chat: a thousand messages arriving in four seconds.

"I know, I know," Maya said, laughing, holding up a hand, "and I love you guys, genuinely. What we have is real." She let that land, let the chat receive it. Then: "It's just, it's a specific kind of real. And I think about that sometimes." She kept it there, didn't pull the thread further. There was a version of that sentence that went somewhere true and useful, and there was a version that turned into content, and she had learned, slowly and not always gracefully, that she couldn't always tell which was which until it was already out of her mouth and in front of fifteen thousand people.

She said it casually, the way you can say true and difficult things when you are in the rhythm of speech and the rhythm is carrying you. The chat received it. The chat always received her.

She played Balatro for two hours after that. She was genuinely good at it, and she lost a run in a way that made her laugh so hard she had to take her hands off the keyboard, and that laugh filled the stream room and the chat erupted and for a long stretch of time she was not aware of herself at all, which was the best thing streaming could do for her and the thing she kept coming back for.

At nine forty-five she said her goodbyes.

"Okay," she said finally, "okay, for real this time. I love you guys. Get some sleep. Drink water. Be nice to someone tomorrow who doesn't expect it." She paused. "Goodnight."

She cut the feed.

The stream room went quiet in the particular way that rooms go quiet when something very loud has just stopped. Not silence exactly. The negative shape of what had been there. The ring light buzzed faintly.

Outside, a car passed on the street, its headlights sliding across the ceiling of the adjacent room in a slow arc, and then it was gone.

Maya sat in the chair for a moment without moving.

The ring light was still on. She reached up and clicked it off.

She was good at this part. The coming down. She had a system for it: ring light off, then the overhead, then the stream clothes into the hamper, then whatever she felt like eating. It was important to have a system.

Without it the end of a stream could sprawl for an hour, checking the clip queue, the comments, the numbers, and then it was midnight and you hadn't eaten and the night had the specific quality of rooms that have held a lot of people and are now empty.

You moved to LA and you built something and the people from before became people you liked on Instagram and that was a kind of connection.

The friend from sophomore year who had screenshotted her texts had found her again a few years ago, slid into the DMs to say she was sorry, that she hadn't understood what she was doing. Maya had written back something warm and meant it. She did not think about it often.

Without the system the end of a stream could sprawl for hours, checking the clip queue, the comments, the numbers, the secondary warmth of the metric, which was not the same as the primary warmth of the room but was available at any hour and asked nothing in return.

### Chapter 3 — Title

The Creator Summit happened every year in February at a hotel in downtown Los Angeles that had been renovated recently enough that it still smelled faintly of fresh paint beneath the cedar diffusers that someone had placed in every corridor in an attempt to make ambition smell like a spa. It was three days of panels and networking and sponsored activations and the particular social pressure of being in a room full of people who were all, simultaneously, performing a version of themselves they hoped would be perceived as the most authentic version of themselves, which was, Derek had noted in his podcast two weeks before attending, the most exhausting possible definition of networking.

He was there because a producer he knew had called in a favor. He did not belong here in the way that belonging here was commercially defined. He was aware of this. He wore it lightly, or tried to.

He had dressed with the care of someone who has good taste and has also learned not to care too much about being seen to have good taste: dark wool trousers, a fitted white Oxford, a vintage blazer from Silver Lake that technically was cut for a woman and fit him exactly right, a detail he would not volunteer. His hair did what it wanted, which he had made peace with in his late twenties.

He spent the first day going to two panels and leaving halfway through both. He ate excellent tacos from a truck outside. He had one genuinely good conversation, with a woman who made documentary content about insect biodiversity, which was the kind of conversation that happened when you stopped looking for the professionally useful conversation and just talked to the next person.

On the second evening there was a reception. Open bar, passed appetizers, a DJ playing something inoffensive at a volume calibrated to require leaning in slightly to be heard. Leaning in creates the physical grammar of intimacy. A deliberate social engineering choice. He got a drink and stood near a column, which was a good place to observe from, and observation was one of the things he did with a room before he decided what he wanted to do with it.

Maya Chen arrived with two other people and there was the slight gravitational shift that happened when someone famous entered a room that was full of people pretending not to notice famous people. Derek noticed it without performing noticing it. He had watched enough of her content that he had the strange double knowledge of knowing her face completely and not knowing her at all, which was the specific epistemology of parasocial relationship, and which he was intellectually clear on even as it produced its predictable small effect.

She looked tired. That was the first thing he noticed. She was dressed simply, a cream blazer over a dark top, and she was performing the social duties of arrival with the practiced ease of someone who had done this a thousand times. But underneath the efficiency there was something, not unhappiness, just a kind of low-grade expenditure, like watching a phone that had been at forty percent battery for a while.

He did not approach her. He got a second drink and drifted toward the balcony because the room was warm and the February night air had a quality of almost remembering something cooler.

She was already there.

Not performing. She was out there alone, not on her phone, just standing at the railing and looking at the skyline, which was doing what the Los Angeles skyline does at night, looking like something assembled without a plan by entities that each had excellent individual taste.

He almost went back inside. But the balcony was large and he was within his rights to stand on it, and so he went to the railing about ten feet away and looked at the skyline from his section of it.

A minute passed.

"It's weird, right?" she said.

He looked over. She was looking at him, not the way you look at someone you recognize in a room but the way you look at someone who is doing exactly what you are doing and you have decided to acknowledge it.

"The skyline?" he said.

"The skyline," she agreed. "It looks so deliberate from here. Like somebody planned it. And then you're in it and it's just a CVS next to a parking structure next to a thing they're definitely calling luxury apartments."

"The view is the lie the city tells about itself," Derek said, and immediately felt the slight internal wince of someone who has said a thing that could read as showing off, and then decided it didn't.

Maya looked at him for a moment. "That's a good line."

"I teach English. I'm required to have a certain line-per-hour output."

She laughed, the real one, the sideways one, the one he recognized from screen. The recognition was strange: knowing the sound of it in advance and then hearing it in three dimensions, where it was exactly the same.

"Are you here as a..."

"Teacher who also podcasts in his apartment to about ninety people," he said. "Derek."

"Maya."

"I know," he said. Then: "I've watched some of your content."

"Some," she repeated, with an expression that was entertained rather than offended.

"A modest and non-obsessive amount," he said. "Your bit about the parasocial thing and your mom. I thought it was honest."

She looked at him for a moment. Most people either asked her how that felt or told her she was brave. He had just said what he thought of it.

"Thanks," she said, with the slight flatness of someone who is used to deflecting compliments and is not sure this was one.

"What do you podcast about?" she asked.

"Film, mostly. But I dress it up as film and then it's usually about something else by the end. Last week I spent forty minutes on Robocop and somehow arrived at a discussion of what institutions do to the parts of people that are inconvenient to them."

"I mean," Maya said, "that's what Robocop is about."

Derek looked at her. He felt the specific and slightly ridiculous pleasure of being understood about a thing.

"Yes," he said. "Exactly. Yes."

"My brother was obsessed with Robocop when we were kids," she said. "I watched it with him like six times. I always thought Murphy's wife was the saddest part. Because she doesn't get him back. She gets the thing that looks like him."

The thing that looks like him. Derek held that for a moment. "That's better than anything I said in forty minutes."

"I had better source material. I had a brother who cried at it." She sipped her drink. "Do you like doing it? The podcast."

"I like the part that's just thinking about movies and then talking about what I think. The part where I have to also be a brand is less interesting to me."

"Yeah," she said, with a quietness that had weight in it.

"How long have you been doing this?" he asked. "The streaming."

"Seven years."

"That's a long time."

"It's most of my adult life," she said. "I'm good at it. I like it. It's just also..." She stopped. Started again. "My mom asked me if I have real friends and I put it in a video because when you don't know how to sit with something, you make it content."

She said it with the same tone she'd used in the video, but here, without the audience, it arrived differently. It arrived as the thing it was.

"That's honest," he said.

"I'm trying to be." She said it and it came out slightly more than she'd meant, and she had a brief impulse to qualify it somehow, and then she didn't because qualifying it would be worse.

He didn't make anything of it. Just nodded once and looked back at the skyline.

"Most people," she said, after a moment, "when they meet me, they either go very casual to prove they're not impressed, or they want to establish who they were before they knew who I was. Some version of I- knew-of-you-but-I-wasn't-affected. You didn't do that."

"I watched some of your content," he said. "It just didn't seem like the most interesting thing about you being here."

She considered that. It was a strange thing to say and she wasn't sure she believed it and she also didn't entirely not believe it.

"I should go back in," she said.

"Probably," he said. Neither of them moved.

"There's a Taiwanese place," she said after a moment. "On Ventura.

They have scallion pancakes that I think you would find unreasonably correct."

"Unreasonably correct is a contradiction in terms," he said. "Either they're correct or they're unreasonable. They can't be both."

"Those are just correct," she said. "There's no affront."

"That's a deeply wrong position and I look forward to arguing it with you at length."

She looked at him. She could tell he meant it, which was different from the usual social performance of meaning it, and she felt the familiar small alarm that went off when something was going to cost her something and she couldn't yet tell what.

"Derek," she said.

"Maya."

"I have eleven million subscribers," she said. "Just so you know what you're getting into, with the arguing at length."

"I have eighty-seven listeners," he said. "We're clearly compatible."

The real laugh came sideways, the way it always did. She didn't try to stop it.

"Scallion pancakes are correct," she said. "And this is not a debate."

"It is absolutely a debate," he said. "It just hasn't started yet."

They stayed on the balcony for another twenty minutes, arguing about Robocop and then the scallion pancake question at greater length than it warranted, and neither of them went back inside until someone came to tell her she was needed for a photograph with a sponsor.

When they separated, she to the photograph and him to his jacket, they had exchanged numbers with a pragmatism that was its own kind of statement. No performance around it. Just: this seems worth continuing.

She did not think about it on the way to the photograph. She thought about it on the way home.

He got his jacket from coat check. Outside, the February night was cool and the city doing its indifferent thing, and he stood on the sidewalk for a moment before calling a car. He got in it. The city went past the windows in its usual way, bright and sprawling and indifferent to individual narratives, the way cities have to be to accommodate so many of them at once.

He was already thinking about what he would say in the next episode.

### Chapter 4 — Title

The school week after the summit was the usual kind of week, which is to say it was relentless in the particular way that other people's needs are relentless when it is your job to meet them. He taught four sections of eleventh grade English and one elective on film, and he was good at all of them, and being good at them cost something, the way any genuine expenditure does, so that by Thursday afternoon he was operating on the specific reserves that experienced teachers learn to locate in themselves, not energy exactly but the memory of energy, which functions almost as well when the students are also tired and just need someone to care about the thing they are supposedly all caring about together.

He drove to Silver Lake after school because he had told himself for two weeks he was going to stop at that record store on Sunset and had not done it yet, and the not-doing had accumulated into a small but genuine weight, the kind that only lifts when you finally just go.

The store was the kind that no longer had any right to exist in the economic sense and did anyway, which Derek respected deeply. Bins of records organized by a logic that was mostly genre and partly the proprietor's personal philosophy. A cat on the counter. The particular smell of old cardboard and vinyl and the dust of other people's afternoons.

He was in the jazz section for twenty minutes before he found it. The Coltrane he had been looking for, not a rare pressing but a specific one, the kind of specific that doesn't make sense to explain to anyone who doesn't already feel it, and he stood there holding it with the particular stillness of a man who has been waiting for something small and is now experiencing the small thing, and it was, in the way these moments are, genuinely and completely good.

He had nobody to text about it. That was the thought that arrived immediately after, uninvited but not unfamiliar, the thought that arrived after every good thing and some of the ordinary things and occasionally at random in the middle of a Tuesday: I want to tell someone. He had the Coltrane in his hands and the afternoon was doing what Los Angeles afternoons did and the record store had the smell of its own and it was, by any available measure, a good moment. He wanted it to land somewhere.

He wanted it to be witnessed, not by fifteen thousand people in a stream, just by one person who would understand what the pressing meant and why he had been looking for it and what it felt like to be standing here holding it.
