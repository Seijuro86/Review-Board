# Anti-Summary Guardrail

If the transcript begins to look like this:

**Wood:** The book works in kitchens.
**Kakutani:** The pacing is late.
**Sehgal:** Trust silence.

Stop and expand.

A real critic turn should include:
- the claim
- the evidence
- the implication
- the disagreement
- the revision consequence

Bad:

**Wood:** The book’s best argument is made in kitchens, not monologues.

Good:

**Wood:** When I say the book’s best argument is made in kitchens, I don’t mean merely that the domestic scenes are charming. I mean that the book repeatedly locates moral knowledge in small acts of attention: who notices someone’s withdrawal, who makes room in a conversation, who mistakes quietness for simplicity. That matters because the larger public-facing machinery of the plot keeps threatening to turn the characters into legible symbols. The kitchen scenes resist that. Kakutani may be right that the middle needs more pressure, but I would be careful about importing pressure from outside if it flattens the inward pressure already present in those rooms.
